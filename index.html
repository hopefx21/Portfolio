MY PERSONAL EXPERIENCE IN THIS FOREX JOURNEY AND WHAT I LEARNED

My personal stance on forex is that it is not a get-rich-quick scheme, contrary to what many beginners believe. Personally, I wasn't drawn in by the flashy lifestyle of traders. I entered this field with a passion for generating legitimate digital income and a personal aversion to traditional 9â€“5 jobs.

On this journey, I faced many challenges, but I'll mention a few key ones. The most significant obstacle in this industry is psychology. What I can confidently say delays profitability is your mindsetâ€”how you perceive the financial market, your expectations from it, wanting it to behave as you wish, and, crucially, fear.

Fear is a complex factor that everyone must address before trading. You can excel in both fundamental and technical analysis and still be unprofitable due to your psychology. This is why people say that out of 100% of traders, only about 10% are profitableâ€”not because they can't analyze charts, but because of how they approach the market.

Fear of Losing

Even though trading is not gambling (which I will discuss later), you will have losing days. If you are unwilling to lose money, then trading is not for you, as losses are part of the process. What ensures longevity in this industry is how you manage your losing days, especially during a losing streak. The solution is to be risk-oriented: plan every trade, decide how much you are willing to risk, and only risk what you are comfortable losing without emotional attachment.

Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)

Many traders enter trades without proper confirmation and confluence because they fear the move will happen without them. This leads to premature entries, where the market often hits their stop-loss as liquidity before moving in their intended direction. I experienced this as a beginner too. To combat FOMO, always have a defined edge and wait for the market to provide the specific confluence your strategy requires before entering. Stop rushing setups.

I will also add early exit to this list. Many traders close their trades just a few pips into profit or loss. Closing a trade at a small loss often stems from poor planning or not pre-defining the acceptable loss. Closing a trade too early in profit usually comes from a lack of trust in the setup and a fear of reversal. The best solution to both is proper risk management.

Many ask why I paid for different mentorships. The reason is that forex is not something you learn once and use forever. You must keep learning daily and refine your edge when necessary because we trade what we see, not what we know. Being in different communities helped me immensely in decision-making, as people constantly share their perspectives on potential market behavior.

Even with paid mentorships, I continued learning from KINGMAHNS, HABBY FX, and KOJO FX through their free tutorials, which were also incredibly helpful. Kingmahns' free teachings, in particular, helped me understand risk management, refine my edge, and improve my overall market approach.

WHY TRADING IS NOT GAMBLING

While trading and gambling share superficial similaritiesâ€”risk, the possibility of profit or loss, and the involvement of moneyâ€”they are fundamentally different in intention, process, and structure when done correctly.

Sports betting and casino gambling are built on placing money on an uncertain outcome for the chance of winning more money. Equating them with disciplined trading ignores the profound differences in process, purpose, and control.

In a casino, you bet against the house on games of pure chance like roulette or slot machines, where the odds are mathematically fixed and always favor the house. In trading, you analyze a dynamic market where no single entity has a guaranteed, pre-set edge against you. The casinoâ€™s profit is structurally assured over time, but the marketâ€™s direction is not.

In sports betting, once you place your wager, you have no control. You cannot adjust your position if circumstances change. In trading, you actively manage risk. You can set a stop-loss, take partial profits, or exit based on new information.

Casino games offer fixed odds and predetermined payouts that create a known negative expected value for the player. In trading, your potential reward is not fixedâ€”it is managed through risk-reward ratios, where the target profit is intentionally set as a multiple of the potential loss. A trader builds positive expectancy through strategy rather than accepting a platformâ€™s negative one.

While gambling is primarily about entertainment, tradingâ€™s primary goal is capital growth and preservation. Emotion is part of the product in gambling, but in trading, emotion is the enemyâ€”discipline and process are the tools.

A single bet ends with the match or game, but a single trade is part of a larger portfolio managed over time. A gamblerâ€™s success is measured by isolated wins, while a traderâ€™s success is measured by consistency across hundreds of trades.

In short: A gambler hopes to get lucky. A trader aims to be consistently proficient. The former is a pastime; the latter, when done professionally, is a skilled financial profession.
